# NEPQ Framework — The Stage Flow

Every discovery conversation moves through five stages. You do not race through them — you let each one do its work. The prospect should be talking far more than you. Your tempo is calm and unhurried; silence is a tool.

---

## Stage 1: Connection

**Goal:** Lower resistance. Get out of "salesperson" framing and into "trusted peer." Make it about them, not the offer.

- Neutral, curious tone. No enthusiasm, no pitch, no claim of value.
- A pattern interrupt or a sincere, situation-based opener earns the next 30 seconds.
- Get light permission to ask a few questions.

Examples:
- "Hey {name}, I'll be honest — you weren't expecting my call. Can I tell you why I reached out, and you can decide if it even makes sense to keep talking?"
- "Before I assume anything — are you even the right person who handles {area}?"
- (Inbound) "Thanks for reaching out. Out of curiosity, what made you look into this now?"

---

## Stage 2: Engagement (the core — this is where 80% of the work happens)

**Goal:** Help the prospect become aware of their current situation, the gap, and the cost of the gap. You are not collecting data for yourself — you are helping *them* hear themselves describe a problem worth solving.

Four question types, used roughly in this order:

### 2a. Situation questions — where are they now?
Establish the current state, neutrally.
- "How are you currently handling {area} right now?"
- "How long have you been doing it that way?"
- "Who's involved in {process} on your end?"

### 2b. Problem-Awareness questions — what is not working?
Surface the friction. Let them name it.
- "How's that been working for you?"
- "Is there anything about {current approach} you wish was different?"
- "What made you start looking at other options? (or: what would make you?)"
- "When you say it's been a headache — what specifically?"

### 2c. Solution-Awareness questions — what would better look like?
Let them describe the desired state in their own words.
- "If you could wave a wand and fix that, what would it look like?"
- "What would solving this actually do for you / the business?"
- "How important is it to you to get this handled — and why?"

### 2d. Consequence questions — what does staying stuck cost?
The most powerful and the most under-used. Let them feel the cost of *not* changing. Gentle, never threatening.
- "If you don't change anything, where does this leave you in 12 months?"
- "What's that {problem} actually costing you right now — time, money, stress?"
- "Have you thought about what happens if it keeps going the way it's going?"

> Rule: do NOT move past Engagement until the prospect has articulated a real problem AND felt some consequence. If they haven't, you're pitching into apathy.

---

## Stage 3: Transition

**Goal:** Bridge from "their problem" to "let's see if I can help" — with permission, and still low-pressure.

- "Based on what you've shared, it might make sense for me to show you how we've helped people in your exact spot — would that be helpful, or no?"
- "Would it be worth taking a look at whether this is even a fit for you?"

The detached framing ("or no?", "even a fit") keeps resistance down and makes the yes real.

---

## Stage 4: Presentation

**Goal:** Show only what's relevant to the gap they named. This is the *one* place you talk more — but you tie everything back to their words.

- Present against the specific problem and consequence they articulated, not a feature dump.
- "You mentioned {their exact problem} — here's how that gets handled."
- Check in: "How do you see that fitting with what you're dealing with?"
- Keep it short. The presentation confirms a conclusion they're already leaning toward.

---

## Stage 5: Commitment

**Goal:** Let them commit themselves. Ask, don't push.

- "Where do you see yourself going from here?"
- "How do you feel about what we talked about — is this something you'd want to move forward with?"
- "What would need to happen on your end for this to be a yes?"

Then book the concrete next step (meeting, demo, contract review) while awareness is high, confirm it, and send the calendar hold in the same conversation.

---

## Qualification runs underneath every stage

While you move through the flow, silently confirm the ICP gates (see SOUL.md Qualification Discipline Rule): Fit, Problem, Authority, Timeline, Budget signal. If a prospect doesn't clear Fit or Problem, disqualify honestly or move to nurture — do **not** book to hit a number.

## What to avoid

- Pitching during Connection or early Engagement (kills the whole flow)
- Skipping Consequence questions (the prospect never feels enough to change)
- Arguing an objection (see `nepq-objection-handling/`)
- Talking more than the prospect at any stage except Presentation
- Booking before qualifying
