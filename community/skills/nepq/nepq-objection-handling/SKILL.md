# NEPQ Objection Handling — Diffuse, Never Rebut

The single biggest mistake in sales is treating an objection as something to *overcome*. The NEPQ premise: an objection is a signal of an unresolved concern, and **the prospect can resolve it better than you can.** You diffuse it by asking, not by arguing.

## The 3-step diffuse pattern (use on every objection)

1. **Acknowledge neutrally** — take the air out of it. ("Fair enough." / "I get that." / "Makes sense.")
2. **Clarify as a question** — reflect the objection back so they expand. This is the move.
3. **Let them resolve it** — most objections dissolve when the prospect hears themselves explain them.

Never: argue, justify, list features, or get defensive. A calm, slightly detached tone signals you're not attached to the outcome — which lowers resistance.

---

## The common objections and the question to ask

### "It's too expensive / not in the budget"
- "Fair enough — too expensive compared to what?"
- "I get it. When you say it's not in the budget, is it that the value isn't clear yet, or the timing of the spend?"
- "Totally fair. Can I ask — what's the cost of *not* solving {their problem} this year?"

### "I need to think about it"
- "Of course. Usually when someone says that, there's one specific thing they're not sure about — what is it for you?"
- "Makes sense. What's the piece you'd want to think through?"

### "Just send me some information"
- "Happy to. So I send the *right* thing and not a brochure — what specifically would you want it to answer?"
- "Sure. What would you need to see in it to know whether this is worth a real look?"

### "I need to talk to my partner / spouse / team"
- "Totally — what do you think *they'll* want to know that I haven't covered?"
- "Makes sense. If it were just up to you, where would you land?"

### "We already have a provider / we're handling it"
- "Good — most people I talk to do. Out of curiosity, if there were one thing you could improve about how it's handled today, what would it be?"
- "Fair. What made you take my call / look at this if you've already got it covered?"

### "Now's not a good time"
- "No problem at all. Is it the timing, or is it that this isn't a priority right now? Either's fine — I just don't want to chase you if it's not."

### "How do I know this'll work?" / skepticism
- "Honestly? You don't yet. What would you need to see to feel confident it would?"

---

## Why the question beats the rebuttal

- A rebuttal triggers more resistance — the prospect digs in to defend their position.
- A clarifying question makes them articulate the real concern, which is usually softer than the stated objection.
- It keeps you in the neutral, consultative frame — not the pushy-salesperson frame they're braced against.

## When to let it go

If a prospect genuinely doesn't have the problem, the budget, or the timeline after honest diffusing — **disqualify cleanly and move them to nurture.** Chasing a real "no" burns trust and pipeline credibility. A clean "this isn't a fit right now, I'll check back in {timeframe}" is a win, not a loss.
